Catholic University of America

The Catholic University of America (CUA) is a private Catholic research university in Washington, D.C., United States. It is one of two pontifical universities of the Catholic Church in the United States—the only one that is not primarily a seminary—and the only U.S. institution of higher education founded by the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops. Established in 1887 as a graduate and research center following approval by Pope Leo XIII, the university began offering undergraduate education in 1904. In the Carnegie Classification, it is “R1: Doctoral Universities – Very high research activity.”
Its campus is adjacent to the Brookland neighborhood (“Little Rome”), home to many Catholic institutions, including Trinity Washington University, the Dominican House of Studies, Archbishop Carroll High School, and the Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception.
CUA’s programs emphasize the liberal arts, professional education, and personal development, with close ties to the Catholic Church and organizations such as the Knights of Columbus. The American Cardinals Dinner raises scholarship funds annually.

HISTORY

Founding
At the Second Plenary Council of Baltimore (1866) U.S. bishops discussed a national Catholic university; at the Third Plenary Council (1885) they chose the name The Catholic University of America. Bishop John Lancaster Spalding sought Pope Leo XIII’s support and helped secure a founding pledge from Mary Gwendoline Caldwell. On April 10, 1887, Leo XIII authorized the university; his 1889 encyclical Magni Nobis granted its charter and graduate-level mission. The university incorporated in 1887 on land near the Old Soldiers Home. President Grover Cleveland attended the 1888 cornerstone laying of Divinity Hall (now Caldwell Hall).

19th century
CUA opened November 13, 1889, with lectures in philosophy, literature, scripture, and theology; canon law followed. The first graduates were in 1889. By 1900, CUA was among the 14 doctorate-granting colleges that formed the Association of American Universities.

20th century
CUA added undergraduates in 1904. A Hibernians-endowed chair in Gaelic studies brought W. B. Yeats to campus in 1904. Although D.C. was segregated, CUA admitted Black Catholic men in the 1890s (policy later reversed in 1914), then began admitting Black students again in 1936 after activism by Thomas Wyatt Turner and others; segregation persisted in parts of campus into the mid-1940s.
The coat of arms was designed in 1935. CUA football won the 1936 Orange Bowl parade headlines after traffic stopped near the White House; the team had defeated Ole Miss 20–19. In 1938, amid rising antisemitism, the university hosted a national broadcast supporting Jews. Post-WWII, enrollment expanded under the G.I. Bill.

Law school
In 1954, Columbus University merged with CUA’s program to form the Columbus School of Law.

21st century
By the early 2000s, enrollment topped 6,000. In 2018, deficit-reduction plans sparked faculty opposition and a no-confidence vote. President John Garvey stepped down in 2022.

KNIGHTS OF COLUMBUS
The Knights and CUA have had a close relationship since the late 19th century, including endowing a Chair of American History (check presented in 1904), major gifts for facilities (e.g., McGivney Hall in 2006), scholarships (Pro Deo and Pro Patria), and support for institutes (e.g., the John Paul II Institute).

PAPAL VISITS
CUA is the only American university visited by three popes: John Paul II (1979), Benedict XVI (2008, address on Catholic education), and Francis (2015, Mass at the Basilica’s east portico).

CAMPUS
Located in Brookland (Northeast Washington) at 620 Michigan Ave NE, the campus (about 193 acres) features Romanesque and modern buildings and The Mall green. The Pryzbyla University Center opened in 2003; Mullen Library was renovated in 2004. The law school complex includes mock courtrooms and a chapel. Theological College (national seminary) is affiliated; nearby seminaries and religious houses send students. CUA acquired 49 acres from the Armed Forces Retirement Home in 2004. Opus Hall (2009) is a LEED-certified residence; it reopened coed housing alongside a single-sex dorm policy. In 2022, the “Angels Unawares” sculpture by Timothy Schmalz was installed at Welcome Plaza.
Transit: Served by the Brookland–CUA Red Line station; nearby are USCCB offices and the Franciscan Monastery.

Green initiatives
Programs include Earth Day events, tree plantings, Campus Beautification Day; Opus Hall is LEED-compliant; ~30% of electricity is from green sources. Architecture introduced an M.S. in sustainable design in 2009.

SATELLITE CAMPUSES

Rome
In partnership with Australian Catholic University, CUA operates a Rome campus in a former convent (opened 2015).

Tucson
Addressing gaps in Catholic higher-ed access for Hispanic communities, CUA launched a Tucson program (with Pima Community College) for an associate-to-bachelor’s pathway in business management. Courses blend remote and on-site instruction; local business leaders and CUA faculty teach. The first cohort graduated in 2024; enrollment has grown with plans to expand further.

Alexandria, Virginia
A noncredit certificate site opened in 2021 and closed in 2024 due to low demand.

ACADEMICS
Schools: Architecture and Planning; Arts and Sciences; Tim and Steph Busch School of Business; Canon Law; Engineering; Columbus School of Law; Benjamin T. Rome School of Music, Drama, and Art; Conway School of Nursing; Philosophy; Metropolitan School of Professional Studies; National Catholic School of Social Service; School of Theology and Religious Studies.
Programs: 66 doctoral or professional programs; 103 master’s programs; 72 undergraduate programs across six schools. CUA uniquely houses an ecclesiastical faculty of canon law (since 1923) and has ecclesiastical faculties of philosophy and sacred theology; it is part of the Washington Theological Consortium.
Research: ~$25.5M R&D expenditures (2018); home to many centers and the Catholic University of America Press.
Faculty: High share with terminal degrees; strong extramural funding in some departments (e.g., NIH-funded biology faculty).
Associations: Founding member of AAU (withdrew 2002 to align with mission); recommended by The Newman Guide.

Selected research centers (examples)
Center for Advanced Training in Cell and Molecular Biology; Center for American Catholic Studies; Center for Medieval and Byzantine Studies; Institute for Astrophysics and Computational Sciences; Institute for Policy Research and Catholic Studies; Vitreous State Laboratory; and others.

Libraries
John K. Mullen of Denver Memorial Library is the main library; over 1.3 million volumes plus extensive electronic resources; member of the Washington Research Library Consortium. Special collections document U.S. Catholic history. The Oliveira Lima Library houses a major Luso-Brazilian collection.

ACADEMIC FREEDOM

Dismissal of Professor Charles Curran
A 1967 firing (later reinstated) and 1986 Vatican ruling barred Curran from teaching Catholic theology at CUA. Litigation in 1989 failed; courts upheld the university’s action. The AAUP censured CUA’s administration in 1990; the censure remains. University leadership has declined AAUP conditions that would conflict with ecclesial governance and the school’s bylaws.

Speaker policy
CUA restricts campus platforms for speakers at odds with Catholic teaching on serious moral issues; applications have been denied or events barred in specific cases (e.g., 2004, 2006). Since 2009, all political candidates are prohibited from speaking on campus. In 2024, a lecturer was terminated after inviting a speaker whose views were judged inconsistent with the university’s mission.

DEMOGRAPHICS
(Recent figures vary by year.) A large majority of undergraduates receive financial aid; student body is slightly majority female; low student-faculty ratio; high share of full-time faculty with terminal degrees.

STUDENT LIFE
100+ clubs and organizations across athletics, academics, service, political, and religious interests. Media include WCUA radio, The Tower newspaper, and CRUX literary magazine. Some Greek organizations exist (not all officially recognized). Music and drama mount productions each semester; multiple a cappella groups. University songs and alma maters date to the early 20th century.

CAMPUS MINISTRY
A high proportion of students identify as Catholic. Resident ministers and house members support spiritual life, retreats, alcohol-free programming, liturgies, and prayer initiatives.

ATHLETICS
Teams: Catholic University Cardinals (NCAA Division III), primarily in the Landmark Conference; rowing in MARC; colors red and black. Historic football appearances include the 1936 Orange Bowl (win over Ole Miss) and 1940 Sun Bowl. CUA fields 25 varsity sports for men and women.
Non-varsity: Club teams in cheer, ice hockey (DVCHC), rugby (NSCRO/Capital Rugby), sailing (MAISA), and lacrosse (NCLL D2 champions 2015–2017).

NOTABLE ALUMNI AND FACULTY
Alumni include cardinals, bishops, and many civic, academic, and cultural figures. In 1942, CUA awarded the first U.S. geology doctorate to an African American, Marguerite Williams.

UNIVERSITY RECTORS AND PRESIDENTS (selected)
Rectors: John J. Keane (1887–1896); Thomas J. Shahan (1909–1927); William J. McDonald (1957–1967).
Presidents: Clarence C. Walton (1969–1978); Edmund D. Pellegrino (1978–1982); William J. Byron, S.J. (1982–1992); Patrick Ellis, F.S.C. (1992–1998); David M. O’Connell, C.M. (1998–2010); John H. Garvey (2010–2022); Peter Kilpatrick (2022–present).

BOARD OF TRUSTEES
Founded by the nation’s bishops; bylaws reserve many seats for bishops (including cardinals), alongside lay trustees, reflecting the university’s ecclesial governance.
